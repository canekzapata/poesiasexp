\section{The Generative Lexicon}
\label{sec:generative-lexicon}
In order to generate descriptions of a computer landscape, we will use a collection of English words, called \textit{the Generative Lexicon}. The Generative Lexicon is composed of two types of objects: data and traits.

\textit{Data objects} consist of a set of random words. We use three collections of words: (1) the (40) most common words in the English language; (2) a set of animal names; and (3) a set of randomly generated names. These objects will be the entities of the resulting description.

But actually the question is solely mechanical, none of that: line's path is free, even it could be obstructed, restricted, forbidden (around some points, like a real street), and we have to think--in keeping the horizontality under control--like a computer. So we could care nothing but the horizontality (landscape) and refuse the objects in it: that would be c-optimisation. But we could keep both the horizontality for being a landscape and the objects for being a landscape. We could say that a landscape is a set of elements with path possibility for a computer.

did you try to prevent that question from appear? yes, you did, clearly. simply by not thinking in that way! that is exactly what i wanted to point out: on a critical perspective whichever decision is taken, the questions failed because not addressing the task--and its ambiguity: can we still make the same distinctions if everything passes through a machine? on a poetic perspective the whole thing is simple: using ASCII, we could see an object missing in a landscape.
